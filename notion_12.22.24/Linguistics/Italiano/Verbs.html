Verbs

  1. Trapassato Remoto (Preterite Perfect) - Generally found in literary or very formal writing, used for an action that occurred before another action in the past, both of which are far removed from the present.
  1. Futuro Anteriore (Future Perfect) - Though not a traditional past tense, it's used for an action that will be completed by a certain future point.

  1. Future In Italian, the future tense for the verb "aggiungere" (to add) is conjugated as follows for each subject pronoun:
  1. Condizionale Presente
  1. Congiuntivo Presente (Present Subjunctive)
  1. Congiuntivo Imperfetto (Imperfect Subjunctive)
  1. Congiuntivo Passato (Past Subjunctive)
  1. Congiuntivo Trapassato (Pluperfect Subjunctive)
  1. Imperativo (Imperative)
  1. Presente Indicativo (Present Indicative)
  1. Presente Congiuntivo (Present Subjunctive) - Used in subordinate clauses that express doubt, possibility, desire, or a subjective view.
  1. Presente Condizionale (Present Conditional) - Although typically associated with future hypotheticals or polite requests, it is a present tense form.
  1. Imperativo (Imperative) - Used for giving orders, advice, or suggestions. Note that the imperative mood lacks the first person singular (io) and third person (lui/lei, loro) forms.
  1. Infinito (Infinitive) - While not conjugated per subject, the infinitive form "aggiungere" is a present tense form.
  1. Participio Presente (Present Participle) - "Aggiungente" is the present participle form, used less commonly and in more complex grammatical constructions.
  1. Gerundio Presente (Present Gerund) - "Aggiungendo" is used similarly to the English present participle ending in -ing, as in "adding".

Each of these forms serves different grammatical functions and expresses various nuances in meaning, particularly in complex sentences or specific contexts.

Introduction to Linguistics

Introduction

The primary five components of Linguistics are:

  1. Syntax
  1. Semantics
  1. Morphology
  1. Phonetics
  1. Phonology

Syntax

Syntax studies the structure of a language. That is, how the different components of speech like nouns, verbs, and adjectives, should come together to render a standard text. One example in the English language is how we use the sequence Subject Verb Object (SVO) in a sentence to deliver a message.

Example: Jesus (subject) threw (verb) the tables (object)

Semantics

Semantics studies the meaning behind a text. In other words, it could be said that semantics studies the meaning of message formatted by the syntax. Or even simpler yet, semantics studies the meaning behind the syntax.

Example: My red door stole a justice above sad

The sentence above is grammatically, and therefore syntactically correct, but it makes no sense. Therefore, though it would pass the syntax test, it would fail the semantics check. We know that the sentence above is grammatically and syntactically correct because the format subject + verb object + preposition + indirect object works if we only input the proper words.

Morphology

It is concerned with the transformation of a word.

Example: How we may form play-er, play-ing, play-ed, play-ful, etc., from the root word ‚Äúplay.‚Äù

Phonetics

Examines the different sounds necessary to speak a language.

Example: The different sounds of each letter in the English alphabet

Phonology

It analyzes the different categories of sounds in a language and how they relate to each other.

Example: Why the "t" in "top" is closer to the "d" in "dog" than to the "t" in "stop.‚Äù

Semantics

Ambiguity

Every language has ambiguity in its words. Two of them are:

  1. homophone: words that sound alike
    • Example: flower ‚Üí flour, bare ‚Üí bear
  1. homonym: words written identically
    • Example: bat (an animal) ‚Üí bat (used in sports), back (preposition) ‚Üí back (part of the body)

Every language also has ambiguity in its structure. For example, am I saying that I shot an elephant while wearing my pajamas? or that I shot an elephant printed on my pajamas?

I shot a deer in my boots

In this given scenario context would be the key. If there is a deer dead on the side of the road but I look fine, the mind of the listener will tilt towards the first option, if on the other hand, there is no deer and I am leaping, then the second one is the most probable.

Side note: God‚Äôs wisdom is revealed in His choice of languages like the Greek which do not allow this level of ambiguity.

Entailment

The truth of one phrase must entail the truth of the second

  • Example: I love to eat ‚Üí at some point I have had the ability to eat
  • Example: My car is fast ‚Üí I have a car and it works

Paraphrase

Two phrases or more that have the same meaning:

Example: I can‚Äôt eat fly ‚Üí it is not possible for humans to naturally fly

Contradictions

Only one phrase can be true. (logical contradiction)

Example: This is a squared circle (impossible)

Gricean Maxisms

They explain the asossiation between a phrase and its meaning. They offer the ‚Äúprinciples for cooperation.‚Äù In other words, how one should talk, semantically speaking. This could be said to be the grammar of semantics. It tells us how to make sense of communication. Below are four Gricean Maxisms:

  1. Maxisim of quality: Be truthful
  1. Maxisim of quantity: Do not use more words than needed
  1. Maxisim of relevance: Maintain the context in your conversations
  1. Maxisim of Manner: Be clear, brief, orderly, and avoid ambuiguity

Implicature

Implicature is found in Phrases with hidden meanings.

Example: You must not have running water at home ‚Üí you have not taken a shower

Presupposition

A phrase that implies knowledge about a certain piece of information

Example: Would you do it again? ‚Üí I know you have done action X before

Very cleverly used in the court system.

Thematic Rules

There are in grammar at least 9 ‚Äúroles‚Äù or ‚Äúliterals.‚Äù

The three most popular are:

  1. Agent: Initiates an event
  • Example: Rodger punched Paul ‚Üí Rodger is the agent (initiator)
  1. Experiencer: The entity experiencing an event.
  • Example: Mina tasted an orange ‚Üí Mina experienced the tasting
  1. Theme or patient: The entity subject to the action
  • Example: Marghetetta was ran over by a chicken ‚Üí Marghetetta is the one suffering the action

The second three most important:

  1. Goal: Point towards a specific entity approaches
  • Example: I am going to the store ‚Üí The store is the final point (my goal)
  1. Recipient: Receiver of an action. Similar to goal
  • Example: The mom nurses the baby ‚Üí the baby is receiving the action of nursing
  1. Source: This is the opposite of goal
  • Example:The mom nurses the baby ‚Üí The Mom is the one giving the action

The last three and the least important:

  1. Location: Place where the action occurs
  • Example: I am checking my email on my phone ‚Üí My phone is the location where the email is being checked
  1. Recipient: Receiver of an action. Similar to goal
  • Example: I am going to measure the room with a ruler ‚Üí the ruler is the instrument
  1. Source: This is the opposite of goal
  • Example: I made a favor to Sue ‚Üí Sue is being benefited by my action

This last one is tricky, but generally is given away by the use of the preposition ‚Äúfor‚Äù
